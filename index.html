What are Biophotons?

Definition

Biophotons (also called Ultraweak Photon Emissions, UPEs) are extremely faint light emitted by living tissue in the UV‚Äìvisible‚Äìnear-visible range (roughly 200‚Äì800 nm).

Intensity: Typically ~1‚Äì1000 photons/cm¬≤/s ‚Äî many orders of magnitude dimmer than normal room light!

Mechanism

Current consensus: Biophotons arise primarily from oxidative metabolic processes:

  • Reactive oxygen species (ROS) in mitochondria and other pathways
  • Excited species like singlet oxygen (¬πO‚ÇÇ) and excited carbonyls (R=O*), especially from lipid peroxidation
  • When these excited molecules relax, they emit photons

Recent Research

Casey et al. (2025, iScience) measured UPEs from human brains through the skull using photomultiplier tubes (PMTs). Key findings:

  • Brain-derived UPEs can be distinguished from room background
  • UPE patterns change with cognitive state (eyes open vs closed, auditory tasks)
  • UPE dynamics do not simply mirror EEG, suggesting they reflect slower, metabolic aspects of brain function

Key Scientific Questions

1. Color / Wavelength

In what wavelength range are biophotons emitted? Does the spectrum change with brain state, or are we just measuring total counts?

2. Intensity

What is the magnitude of photon emission (~photons per cm¬≤ per second)? Why are such emissions not visible and require ultra-sensitive detectors?

3. Mechanism

Which biochemical processes produce the light (ROS, mitochondria, lipid peroxidation)? Why are these emissions considered non-thermal?

4. Light vs Heat

UPEs are distinct from thermal radiation:

  • Different wavelength range (visible/near-visible vs IR)
  • Different dependence on oxidative metabolism vs temperature

5. Electromagnetic Environment

Do static charges or low-frequency electrical fields emit photons that contaminate measurements? Why typical room EM fields (radio/low-frequency) are not directly detected by visible-band PMTs.

6. Instrumentation Drift

How sensor sensitivity can drift over time. Need for:

  • Dark-count baselines (no subject)
  • Background detectors facing away from head
  • Randomized task order
  • Possible calibration light source

7. Biological Confounds

Background UPE from skin, hair, scalp, and microbes. We measure total photons from the head region, not "pure brain" photons, and rely on task-dependent changes and head vs background comparisons.

8. Environmental Light Confounds

Major extraneous variables: light leaks, windows, monitors, phones, LEDs. Mitigation requires light-tight rooms, no displays, and minimal personnel.

Experimental Design

Detectors

  • 3‚Äì4 photomultiplier tubes (PMTs) or single-photon-sensitive detectors
  • Spectral sensitivity ~350‚Äì700 nm (visible/near-visible)
  • Outputs: photon counts per time bin (e.g., counts per 100 ms)

Geometry

  • Head detectors: At least two PMTs near occipital region (visual cortex) and temporal region(s) (auditory cortex)
  • Background detectors: 1‚Äì2 PMTs pointing away from head to measure room background

Environment

  • Dark room with no windows or fully blacked-out windows
  • Doors sealed (no visible light leaks)
  • All monitors/LEDs off and covered
  • Optional very dim red setup light (turned off during data collection)

Conditions per Participant

  1. Dark adaptation: 10‚Äì15 minutes in the dark
  2. Task blocks (each ~2 minutes, randomized order):
    • Eyes closed, relaxed (alpha-dominant EEG expected)
    • Eyes open, fixating in darkness
    • Auditory task (e.g., detect oddball tones)
    • Empty-chair baseline (no subject) at start and end
Sampling rate: Photon counts sampled at 10 Hz or higher (e.g., 100-ms or 1-s bins)

Measurement Challenges

Signal Characteristics

  • Very low count rates (tens of counts per second per detector)
  • Poisson-like stochastic processes
  • Slow fluctuations (<1 Hz) mimicking metabolic rhythms
  • Subtle task-dependent changes (small percentage differences)

Analysis Considerations

  • Head vs Background: Compare head detectors to background detectors to isolate brain-specific effects
  • Drift Correction: Use empty-chair baselines to detect and correct for instrument drift
  • Frequency Analysis: Power spectra reveal slow components (<1 Hz) that may change with task
  • Statistical Robustness: Need careful comparison to avoid false effects from drift or non-randomized conditions
‚ö†Ô∏è Common Pitfall: A naive analysis ignoring drift or background can give misleading results. Always compare head vs background and account for baseline drift!

Here are three metaphors showing this equation-solving process, ranked from most illustrative:

ü•á Most Illustrative: Grocery Store Checkout

You buy apples ($2 each) and oranges ($3 each). The cashier says: "$11 total and you bought 4 fruits." You instantly know: 3 apples + 1 orange = $11. If they said "2 fruits total," you'd have infinite solutions. The checkout gives you 2 equations (total cost + total items) with 2 unknowns (apple + orange count) - perfect degrees of freedom for a unique solution!

ü•à Color Mixing for Paint

To match a target color, you mix red and blue paints. You measure how much red and blue light the mixture absorbs. With absorption data at two wavelengths, you can solve for the amounts of each paint. If both paints absorbed light identically, you'd have a "zero determinant" situation - no unique solution possible.

ü•â Two Scales Problem

You weigh an object on two different scales. Scale A reads 5kg, Scale B reads 3kg. You know Scale A is accurate but Scale B has a 2kg offset. With these two measurements and knowledge of the offset, you can solve for the true weight. This shows how multiple measurements with known relationships let you separate unknowns.
